education during the pandemic

The global disruption to education caused by the COVD-19 pandemic is unprecedented, with serious learning consequences. More than 1.6 billion students were affected by school closures as a result of the crisis, which brought education systems around the world to a halt. While nearly every country in the world provided students with remote learning opportunities, the quality and scope of these programs varied greatly, and they were only partial substitutes for in-person learning. Schools are still closed for millions of children and youth nearly two years later, and millions more are at risk of never returning to school. Evidence of the negative effects of school closures on children's learning reveals a harrowing reality: Learning losses are substantial, with the most marginalized children and youth disproportionately affected.


Evidence from countries like Brazil, Pakistan, India, South Africa, and Mexico shows significant declines in math and reading skills, which are sometimes roughly proportional to the length of school closures. There was variation across countries, as well as by subject, socioeconomic status, gender, and grade level of students regarding how students were affected by the pandemic. For example, younger students, particularly pre-schoolers, had less access to remote learning and were more affected by learning loss than their older counterparts. Evidence from around the world suggests that the pandemic has exacerbated educational inequities, with low-income children, people with disabilities, and girls being less likely to access remote learning.

education inequities worsen

1. Gender was a driver of inequality in some countries. Girls often have restricted access to devices and the internet, as well as a lack of ICT or information and communications technologies knowledge and skills, limiting their capability to benefit from remote learning. In some countries and communities, social norms may create inequalities for girls to use digital devices. Gendered demands on girls' time, such as increased responsibilities at home, also obstruct their ability to engage in remote learning and result in less time for schoolwork.

However, emerging research from Ethiopia, India, and Vietnam shows that there are no substantial variations in the distant learning experiences of boys and girls, and that uneven remote learning experiences are more likely to be driven by the household's socioeconomic level. In Peru, male students were less likely to participate in distant learning than female students.

2. The digital divide became blatantly apparent, with millions of children left behind as the shift to remote learning took place. Many countries adopted technological solutions that were not well suited to their contexts as a result of school closures, creating a "remote learning paradox." Rural households are systematically less likely to have internet. Digital divides between urban and rural communities were greatest in Eastern and Southern Africa, East Asia and the Pacific, and Latin America and the Caribbean.

Even within high-income countries, disparities across household income were evident. In ten European countries, fourth grade students from low socioeconomic backgrounds were half as likely as their more affluent peers to have internet access at home. Countries with longer school closures had lower rates of school-aged children with internet available at home, affecting access to remote learning if other modalities were not available or utilized.

3. Students with disabilities faced challenges in engaging in distance learning. If remote learning modalities, such as sign language interpretation, closed captioning, or Braille, are not adequately tailored, students with disabilities are disproportionately excluded. Only 33% of low-income countries indicated they had taken steps to assist students with disabilities. A global survey of students with disabilities, their teachers, and their parents or caregivers highlighted a variety of environmental, educational, and health-related challenges that students with disabilities faced at the start of the pandemic and during school closures, as partially recorded above.
